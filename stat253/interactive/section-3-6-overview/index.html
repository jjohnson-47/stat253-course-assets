‚ö° The Multiplicative Rule

Multiplicative Rule of Probability

Purpose: Calculate the probability of the intersection of two events (both events occurring)

Formula:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) = P(B) \cdot P(A \mid B)$$

This is just a rearrangement of the conditional probability formula!

Key Insight

Starting from: $P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$

Multiply both sides by $P(A)$:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)$$

Translation: "Probability of both" = "Probability of first" √ó "Probability of second given first"

üî¨ Scientific Example: Milgram Psychology Study

Classic Psychology Research

In Stanley Milgram's famous obedience experiments from the 1960s, researchers studied teacher behavior when asked to shock learners for wrong answers. Define these events:

  • A: Teacher applies severe shock (450 volts)
  • B: Learner protests verbally before receiving shock

Research findings:

  • P(B) = 0.5 (50% of learners protest verbally)
  • P(A | B) = 0.7 (70% of teachers still apply severe shock even when learner protests)

Research Question: What's the probability that both events occur (learner protests AND teacher applies severe shock)?

üìä Applying the Multiplicative Rule

1 Identify what we want:
We need P(A ‚à© B) = P(learner protests AND teacher shocks)
2 Apply multiplicative rule:
P(A ‚à© B) = P(B) √ó P(A | B)
P(A ‚à© B) = 0.5 √ó 0.7 = 0.35
3 Interpret the result:
About 35% of the time, learners will protest verbally AND teachers will still apply severe shock

üéØ Key Finding: 35% of cases involve both protest and severe shock

This demonstrates the disturbing tendency for authority figures to persist even when subjects show distress.

üîç Why This Matters

The multiplicative rule helps researchers quantify the co-occurrence of behaviors in controlled experiments. This particular result was crucial in understanding obedience to authority and ethical considerations in research.

üéØ Understanding Independence

Independent Events

Definition: Events A and B are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other.

Mathematical test: A and B are independent if and only if:

$$P(A \mid B) = P(A) \text{ and } P(B \mid A) = P(B)$$

Equivalent condition:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$$

‚úÖ Independent Events

Examples:

  • Coin tosses
  • Card color & face card status
  • Medical test results on different patients
  • Rolling dice multiple times

Formula: P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B)

‚ùå Dependent Events

Examples:

  • Drawing cards without replacement
  • Weather on consecutive days
  • Sports performance ("hot" streaks)
  • Stock prices over time

Formula: P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B | A)

üß† Key Insights About Independence

  1. Can't be determined from Venn diagrams: You must calculate!
  2. Mutually exclusive events are dependent: If A and B can't both occur, then P(A|B) = 0 ‚â† P(A)
  3. Independence simplifies calculations: P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B)
  4. Common misconception: Independent ‚â† mutually exclusive
  5. Practical importance: Many statistical methods assume independence

üé≤ Counter-Example: Dependent Card Drawing

Drawing Without Replacement

Now consider drawing two cards from a deck WITHOUT replacing the first card:

  • A: First card is an Ace
  • B: Second card is an Ace

Question: Are these events independent?

üìù Dependence Analysis

1 Calculate P(A):
P(first card is Ace) = 4/52 = 1/13
2 Calculate P(B | A):
P(second Ace | first was Ace) = 3/51
(3 Aces left in 51 remaining cards)
3 Calculate P(B | A^c):
P(second Ace | first was not Ace) = 4/51
(4 Aces left in 51 remaining cards)
4 Test independence:
P(B | A) = 3/51 ‚â† P(B | A^c) = 4/51
Since these conditional probabilities are different, A and B are dependent!

‚ùå Clear Dependence

The first draw affects the second!

If first card is an Ace: P(second Ace) = 3/51 ‚âà 0.059

If first card is not an Ace: P(second Ace) = 4/51 ‚âà 0.078

Knowing the first result changes the probability of the second

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

üö´ What NOT to confuse:

  • Independent ‚â† Mutually Exclusive: Independent events CAN occur together
  • P(A ‚à© B) ‚â† P(A | B): Intersection probability vs. conditional probability
  • P(A | B) ‚â† P(B | A): Order matters in conditional probability
  • Independence ‚â† Causation: Independence doesn't mean one causes the other

‚úÖ Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify the events clearly
  2. Check if independence is stated or must be tested
  3. For independent events: Use P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B)
  4. For dependent events: Use P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B | A)
  5. Always interpret results in context

‚úÖ Key Takeaways

Master These Concepts:

  1. Multiplicative Rule: P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B | A)
  2. Independence test: P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B)
  3. Independence meaning: One event doesn't affect the other
  4. Simplification: Independence makes calculations easier
  5. Real applications: Medical trials, quality control, scientific experiments
  6. Common patterns: Sampling with/without replacement
  7. Verification: Always check your independence assumptions
